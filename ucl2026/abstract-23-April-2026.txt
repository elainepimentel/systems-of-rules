Higher-level rules for sequent calculus

Dale Miller
Inria-Saclay & LIX, Institut Polytechnique de Paris

Theories within a base logic are traditionally represented either as
collections of formulas (axioms or assumptions) or as inference rules
bypassing logical connectives. Schroeder-Heister and Avron established
an equivalence between conventional natural deduction and higher-level
rules for intuitionistic logic, which the LF framework later
generalized using the dependently typed lambda-calculus. Transitioning
to sequent calculus facilitates higher-level rules for both classical
and intuitionistic sequent proof systems. While replacing geometric
formulas (bipoles) with rules is well established, we investigate what
can be done for non-geometric axioms or assumptions.

Building on this foundation, we show that the "system of rules"
approach, where inference rules introduce scoped rules into premises,
is a direct interpretation of polarized proof systems. By focusing on
polarized formulas, such as those for least upper bounds, we obtain
synthetic inference rules in which premise assumptions correspond to
specific, scoped rules. The structure of these rules is determined by
whether the predicate polarity is positive or negative. Furthermore,
we describe how Tseitin predicate symbols can be utilized to
transition these scoped rules into a global context.

This talk is based on an abstract delivered to the Proof Society
Workshop in September 2024 and is joint work with Elaine Pimentel.
